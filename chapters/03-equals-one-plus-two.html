    01 — More Calculus (yay!)

    Let's say: $ \ {y \ = \ f (x)} $
    Then let's say we increase $ x $ by an infinitesimal amount $ dx \ $; $ y $ will receive a corresponding variation $ dy $ (increase or decrease, depending on $ f $).
    Then:
    $$ f (x + dx) \ = \ y + dy \ = \ f (x) + dy \quad \iff \quad dy \ = \ f (x + dx) - f (x) $$
    Let's call back our snail from Chapter ii.
    If $ x $ is time and $ y $ is distance, then $ dy $ is: how much distance the snail has covered during the time $ dx $.
    So $ dy $ is a distance. Its value is dependent upon the duration of $ dx $.
    So if we want the rate of change of $ y $ (the speed of the snail), we need to normalise $ dy $ relatively to $ dx $.
    That is: we don't want $ dy $, we want $ dy $ per $ dx $ — whose shortened notation is: $ \ dy / dx \ $.
    The differential operator "$ d $" is both in the numerator and the denominator, so it kind of cancels out (?). Which is why we're allowed to define speed as "distance over time" without mention of infinitesimal increase (don't quote this one).
    If:
    $$ dy \ = \ f (x + dx) - f (x) \quad $$
    then:
    $$ \frac{dy}{dx} \ = \ \frac{f (x + dx) - f (x)}{dx} $$

    02 — What is sound?

    Sound is a variation of pressure propagating through matter.
    When this matter is in contact with our ears (usually air or water) and our brain notices it, it's called hearing.
    We've seen, with Fourier, that this phenomenon is always decomposable in an infinite sum of partials, called spectrum.
    Each partial is a sinusoid. So every time a variation of pressure propagates through matter, the phenomenon can be expressed as periodic oscillations — infinite sum means all frequencies are always present if we admit that their amplitude can be zero.
    We don't necessarily hear identical repeated patterns and yet that's all we ever hear. Unless their frequency is too low (infrasonic) or too high (ultrasonic).
    Random sounds are deterministic: digital white noise is generated by randomising each sample and yet it produces all frequencies, that is: oscillations of fixed period.
    Spinoza wrote (Ethics) that everything has a cause and that "random" only means "of ignored cause".
    Reject this principle and no science is left.
    If there's movement, there's energy. Energy takes many forms and is always convertible (although modern physics is currently stuck with entropy).
    Taking advantage of this convertibility is what analog circuits do: variations of pressure become electromagnetic variations.
    But the movement is reproduced, hence the name "analogue" meaning equivalent in a different form.
    Therefore Fourier's decomposition applies to analog signals. Any voltage has a spectrum. If it's a pure DC offset (constant voltage), then all partials have an amplitude of zero except the one whose frequency is zero.
    But the integral is valid.
    We've seen (Chapter i) that the inverse Fourier transform is a special case of the inverse Laplace transform — so not all Laplace is Fourier, but all Fourier is Laplace.
    Then the inverse Laplace transform can describe any analog signal.
    Whatever we feed our circuit, and whatever comes out, will oscillate at all frequencies (remember: zero is a number).
    So if we focus on one isolated partial at the input, we know it will be present at the output. Phase and amplitude might not be the same (amplitude might be zero) but all frequencies are always everywhere.
    Even absolute silence is just all amplitudes equal to zero.
    That's right: silence is sound — sometimes music even: ever listened to Franz Schubert?
    (In French, a crotchet rest is called a "soupir". It means "whisper".)
    A black picture is a picture. Silence is black sound.

    04 — Pasghetti

    Some silly people do not enjoy hardcore mathematics.
    If you're one of them, you can breathe now: we're about to doodle our path around.
    Back to the capacitor.
    Let's simplify the notation:
    • $ {x \ = \ X (s) \exp (s t)} \ \ \ \ $ [input]
    • $ {y \ = \ Y (s) \exp (s t)} \ \ \ \ $ [output: voltage across C]
    Then:
    $$ y \ = \ \int\nolimits \omega_c (x - y) $$
    where we got rid of the terms which contributed to DC ($ t_0 $ and $ \ y (t_0) \ $).
    The integration starts whenever and always ends right now. The differential is time ($ dt $ or $ d\tau $) but now it's implied.
    This is not a valid expression.
    But it helps reduce the signal path to its core elements.
    Let's decompose the formula:
    • $ \alpha \ = \ x - y $
    • $ \beta \ = \ \omega_c \ \alpha $
    • $ \gamma \ = \ \int\nolimits \beta $
    Almost there. But $ \ \int\nolimits \ $?
    Chain rule and the fact that $ \ \exp (x) \ $ is its own derivative give: $ \ {d \exp (s t) / dt \ = \ s \exp (s t)} \ $.
    So if our input is a Laplace partial (complex sinusoid), multiplying by $ s $ is differentiating — or: $ s $ is a differentiator.
    (Didn't just coin the term: Wikipedia )
    Then dividing by $ s $ (or multiplying by $ 1/s $) is the reciprocal to differentiation.
    You might have not noticed but in section #01 of this very chapter (while looking for something else), we've proven that this reciprocal is integration.

    Short recap:

    $ df $ is how much $ \ f (x) \ $ varies when $ dx $ is added to $ x $,
    so if you stack all $ df $ from the beginning of $ \ f (x) \ $'s domain up to $ \ x = a \ $, you get $ \ f (a) \ $, and since $ \ df = df/dx \ \cdot \ dx \ $, stacking means integrating $ \ df/dx \ $ with respect to $ x $.
    So $ 1/s $ is an integrator.
    (Again: didn't just coin the term: Wikipedia )
    Then, assuming input is a Laplace spectrum and the circuit is LTI, we can safely substitute integrals with divisions by $ s $.
    That's right: our friend Laplace turned Calculus into basic arithmetic.
    Guess his integral is not just beautiful.
