    01 — Do I know what a filter is?

    Being a caffeine addict, I should certainly hope so.
    Some people enjoy Turkish coffee. Good for them. I don't.
    I like the grains in my cup to be under a certain size. And I choose my coffee filters accordingly.
    Fine grains go in my cup, coarse grains go in the compost.
    The sum of the fine grains in my cup and the coarse grains in the compost is equal to the unfiltered coffee.
    Maybe the coffee paradigm is not as irrelevant as it would seem (Turkish people might disagree...).
    We've written Chapter I together, we know what a spectrum and its partials are.
    Let unfiltered coffee be the name of a spectrum and grain be an alias of partial.
    Now what could coarse and fine possibly mean?
    All partials can very well have amplitude $ = 1 $ and phase $ = 0 $, the spectrum remains a spectrum.
    Their frequency is their identity. Their frequency is linked by definition to their period.
    So coarse could mean either high frequency or long period.
    And since we're free (aren't we?) we can assign the pointer to what feels the most intuitive.
    Frequency is abstract (speed is not something you can see, it's the first derivative of something you can see); while a period is very palpable on a plot.
    I'll assign coarse to long period and fine to short period. Pull a request if you disagree.
    It would mean that frequencies under a threshold set to my taste would end up in the compost.
    And the filter would remain the filter.
    But how would such a filter work? How do we separate velocities?
    I can think of a way, but if you don't like my parables... well bad news.

    Second paradigm: Lost in the woods.

    A group of people is lost in the woods. No known destination, just moving forward towards the unknown.
    It's heavily raining and they're not dressed adequately.
    They reach a crossroad.
    • One path is not covered by trees, the rain falls upon it.
    • One path has a gap over which you can jump on the condition that you're fast enough.
    Everyone wants to take the path they consider the easiest.
    For fast people it means no rain. ($ := $ group H) For slow people it means no gap. ($ := $ group L)
    The group splits. I've filtered people based on their velocity.
    Notice that group H $ + $ group L $ = $ initial group.
    Now replace people with electric potential, rainy path with Resistor and gaped path with Capacitor.
    You get what is called an $ RC $ circuit .

    Edit

    This is mainly bullshit, as the rest of the chapter will reveal.
    We learn from our mistakes, don't we? Well I've learnt a lot here!
    Which path leads to the ground (compost) and which to the output (coffee cup) depends on whether you want a filter that discards low frequencies and lets high frequencies pass (High-Pass filter); or one that discards high frequencies and lets low frequencies pass (Low-Pass filter).
    An $ RC $ circuit is an electrical circuit. What is electricity?

    04 — Electrical breakdown

    Connecting our pseudo-prismatic sandwich to $ A $ and $ B $ would look like:
    A o----------
    |
    [ conductive_base_α ]
    [=====insulator=====]
    [ conductive_base_β ]
    |
    B o----------
    Let's define:
    $$ U_A $$
    the electric potential of $ A $
    $$ U_B $$
    the electric potential of $ B $
    $$ U_{\alpha} $$
    the electric potential of base $ \alpha $
    $$ U_{\beta} $$
    the electric potential of base $ \beta $
    $$ U_{bd} $$
    the breakdown voltage of the insulator.
    Initial state
    $$ \ \longrightarrow \quad U_{\alpha} = U_{\beta} $$

    First case: $ \qquad U_B - U_A \ \geq \ U_{bd} $

    This is the easy one: an electrical breakdown will allow a current to flow through the insulator.
    The previously infinite Resistance will be broken to some finite value ($ R $) and the system drawn by the schematic would be equivalent to a resistor between $ A $ and $ B $. If we know $ R $, Ohm's law gives us $ \ I \ = \ {(U_B - U_A) / R} \ $.
    The electric field travels from $ B $ to $ A $, crossing the insulator. Equilibrium is achieved ($ U_B = U_A $), the electrical breakdown ends.
    The total voltage is zero and the insulator is an insulator again (probably damaged though).

    Second case: $ \qquad U_B - U_A \ \leq \ U_{bd} $

    In nodal analysis, an ideal cable is considered equivalent to a node (segment $ \approx $ point), because the electric field is so fast that its displacement can be approximated as instantaneous for practical uses. Let's allow this assumption.
    As soon as we connect our tripartite anonymous toy, the electric field gets (temporarily) dissipated.
    The potential energy of $ A $ is transferred to electrons across the ideal cable.
    Voltage is not energy, it's energy per charge. So $ U_A = U_{\alpha} $ will take time.
    In our current example this time is undefined because it relies on $ I $ (intensity of the current $ = $ charge per time) which is inversely proportional to $ R $ and therefore relies (here) on a division by zero (since $ R = 0 $).
    Never mind, let's assume it's ok.
    Remember: if electrons can move, they will. Between $ A $ and $ \alpha $ they can, so they will.
    The electric charge will accumulate on $ \alpha $. The initial potential energy at $ A $ is now kinetic energy in the ideal cable, progressively converting back into potential energy at $ \alpha $.
    $ U_{\alpha} $ is increasing.
    Now let's break on through to the other side.
    Remember: the energy in one charge carrier is dependent upon the other charge carriers that surround it.
    So $ \alpha $ charging will affect $ \beta $. There will be an electric field across the insulator whose strength is dependent upon $ \alpha $'s and $ \beta $'s surface and the distance between them: the more charge carriers are facing each other and the closer they are, the more energy per charge.
    Or, thinking backwards: the charge required to reach a certain voltage across the insulator grows with the bases' surface and proximity.
    So now there's a vector in $ \alpha $ and an identical one in $ \beta $.
    "Electrons can move" means (1) they're urged to and (2) the path is clear.
    The path between $ \beta $ and $ B $ satisfies both conditions.
    Then whatever happens at $ \alpha $ will be reflected at $ B $.
    Then $ \alpha $ charging will result in $ \beta $ discharging in equal proportion: electrons will drift towards $ B $.
    (By "charging" I mean "negatively charging". I could mean "positively charging", then the words "charging" and "discharging" would have to switch places. They're interchangeable concepts based on your test charge — which I took negative, which is not what you're supposed to do... Sorry, I'm bad at following rules.)

    Important clarification — I had a really hard time solving this:

    Aporia:
    The description above makes it look like there is a linear causal chain from $ A $ to $ B $ where $ \alpha $ charges ergo $ \beta $ discharges. Period.
    Working on some later part of this chapter, I realised that this model isn't sufficient.
    In short: what happens between $ \beta $ and $ B $ influences what happens between $ A $ and $ \alpha $.
    It looked like causality went circular — that is: breaking its own laws.
    A consequence affecting its cause... Zero delay feedback loops — in a filter? why not — in my brain? no thanks.
    It seemed unfair: I do my best to not mention anything that would distract us from the point and Quantum physics strikes unannounced?
    Solution:
    There is indeed a circularity, but it's not instantaneous; it just happens really fast.
    The repartition of the charge inside matter is responsible for potential energy. Potential energy creates electric fields.
    Electric fields make the charge move. REPEAT.

    Also "electric fields" (plural) got me confused. An electric field is a vector. Addition can be performed on vectors.
    So it would probably be more intuitive to consider the total electric field of the system.
    Which both reacts to and generates movement, and has a magnitude and a direction but could have its origin anywhere between $ A $ and $ B $.
    Everything starts with particles attracting and repelling each other. Their repartition shapes electricity.
    So when they move, electricity changes shape.

    The tendency towards equilibrium is constant.
    So $ \alpha $ starts charging, then $ \beta $ starts discharging, this allows $ \alpha $ to keep charging, which makes $ \beta $ keep discharging, etc [c].

    The whole confusion came from a universal axiom: $ \alpha $ and $ \beta $ always have the same absolute charge and opposite polarities.
    This is false.
    There is a back-and-forth causal alternation during the charge. The fact that it's close to the fastest speed we know doesn't make it literally instantaneous.
    If you're neurotypical, you must think I'm stupid right now.
    The French call this enculer des mouches, which literally translates to fucking flies in the arse — classy, right?
    It means nit-picking for its own sake.
    This "obvious" approximation took a full day of my life that I'm never recovering. Not a game here.
    Full picture:
    • current between $ A $ and $ \alpha $
    • no current between $ \alpha $ and $ \beta $
    • current between $ \beta $ and $ B $
    Until enough electric charge from $ A $ has drifted to $ \alpha $ so that $ \ {U_A = U_{\alpha}} \ $;
    and enough electric charge from $ \beta $ has drifted to $ B $ so that $ \ {U_B = U_{\beta}} \ $.
    Which means that once the charge has finished drifting, $ \ {U_B - U_A} \ = \ {U_{\beta} - U_{\alpha}} \ $.
    Or: the voltage between $ A $ and $ B $ has decreased, the one between $ \alpha $ and $ \beta $ has increased; and now they're equal to one another.
    The bases $ \alpha $ and $ \beta $ are called plates.
    The insulator is called the dielectric. All three together form a condenser (or, more commonly, a capacitor).
    Condenser is obsolete in English (except when talking microphones). Condensateur is still the French standard.
    The dielectric has a breakdown voltage $ U_{bd} $ dependent on its composition and its geometry.
    The plates have a maximum charge $ Q_{max} $ dependent on their geometry — but also on the dielectric's breakdown voltage.
    Indeed, imagine there's room for $ x $ Coulombs (charge) on each plate, depending on the overall geometry this means $ \chi $ Volts (voltage).
    If $ \ \chi \gt U_{bd} \ $ it will never be reached: electrical breakdown will always happen first.
    $ Q_{max} $ determines how many charge carriers face each other at once. So a higher $ Q_{max} $ means more potential energy per charge which means more maximum voltage across the dielectric. $ \quad \longrightarrow \ {Q_{max} \ = \ k \cdot \chi} $
    But $ \chi $ might very well be unreachable, so $ U_{bd} $ is a more useful constant. Let's redefine:
    $$ k \ = \ Q_{max} / U_{bd} $$
    The constant $ k $ seems quite useful. What would its dimension be? $ \quad \longrightarrow \quad $ Charge per voltage.
    So "how much charge contributes to voltage" or "how much charge is needed to reach a certain voltage".
    Its actual name is Capacitance and its usual symbol is $ C $. $ Q_{max} $ and $ U_{bd} $ are extreme cases, the general one is:
    $$ C \ = \ q / U \qquad ; \qquad q \ = \ C \cdot U \qquad ; \qquad U \ = \ q / C $$
    where $ q $ is the absolute value of the charge of either of the plates and $ U $ is the voltage across the dielectric.
    (An ideal capacitor has a constant capacitance.)
    These parenthesis are doing harm, this is important: $ \quad C \ $ is a constant.
    And it relies on the dielectric's dielectric strength, which relies on composition and geometry.
    Composition can be paper, ceramic, air, vacuum... anything with a breakdown voltage (preferably not too low).
    Geometry: $ C $ increases with the area of the plates and decreases with the thickness of the dielectric.

    Appendix — DC + $ RC $

    Edit

    In what follows we'll be using some of the rules of differentiation .
    If you want proofs that they're valid, hold your breath until next chapter.
    Let's simplify the nomenclature of our equation by making the substitutions:
    $$ f (x) $$
    $$ \longleftarrow \qquad U_C (t) $$
    $$ f_0 $$
    $$ \longleftarrow \qquad U_C (0) $$
    $$ k $$
    $$ \longleftarrow \qquad 1/RC $$
    $$ a $$
    $$ \longleftarrow \qquad U_A (t) $$
    Later: $ \ U_A \ $ (a constant over time doesn't need to be expressed as a function of time).
    Then:
    $$ df/dx \ = \ k \bigl( a - f (x) \bigr) $$
    That's just equation [1] $$ \frac{d U_C}{dt} \ = \ \frac{U_A (t) - U_C (t)}{RC} $$ after the substitutions.
    Let $ \ g (x) \ = \ a - f (x) $
    Then: $ \ {dg/dx} \ = \ {d (a - f) / dx} \ = \ {(da - df) / dx} $
    $ a $ is a constant so $ \ {da = 0} \ $.
    Then: $ \ {dg / dx} \ = \ {- df / dx} \quad \iff \quad {df / dx} \ = \ {- dg / dx} \ $
    So:
    $$ - dg / dx \ = \ k g (x) \quad \iff \quad dg / dx \ = \ - k g (x) $$
    We know that $ \ \exp (x) \ $ is its own derivative, then according to the chain rule :
    $$ d \exp (-k x) / dx \ = \ -k \cdot \exp (-k x) $$
    which is equivalent to: $ \quad {dg / dx} \ = \ {-k \cdot g (x)} \quad $ if we let $ \quad {g (x)} \ = \ {\exp (-k x)} \ $.
    So:
    $$ a - f (x) \ = \ \exp (-k x) \quad \iff \quad f (x) \ = \ a - \exp (-k x) $$
    Reversing the substitutions:
    $$ U_C (t) \ = \ U_A - \exp (-t / RC) $$
    This gives: $ \quad {U_C (0)} \ = \ {U_A - 1} \quad $ while it should be $ f_0 $ which should be independent from $ U_A $.
    Back to "$ \ {dg / dx} \ = \ {-k g (x)} \quad $ if we let $ \quad {g (x)} \ = \ {\exp (-k x)} \ $" ...
    We might have been a bit quick to conclude from "this is a solution" to "this is the only solution".
    Indeed, according to the constant factor rule:
    $$ d C \exp (-k x) / dx \ = \ -k C \exp (-k x) $$
    which also satisfies: $ \quad dg / dx \ = \ -k g (x) $
    Then we need to give a known value to the constant $ C $. Instead of hoping for it to provide $ f_0 $, let's make it a condition.
    Then: $ \quad {C \exp (-k \cdot 0)} \ = \ {a - f_0} \quad \iff \quad C \ = \ {a - f_0} $
    Then: $ \quad g (x) \ = \ {(a - f_0) \exp (-k x)} $
    Then:
    $$ a - f (x) \ = \ (a - f_0) \exp (-k x) \quad \iff \quad f (x) \ = \ a - (a - f_0) \exp (-k x) $$
    Reversing the substitutions (again):
    $$ U_C (t) \ = \ U_A - \bigl( U_A - U_C (0) \bigr) \exp (-t / RC) $$
    $$ \begin{align} \text{then:} \quad && U_C (0) \ = \ & U_C (0) \quad & \longrightarrow \ \text{check.} & \\ \text{and:} \quad && U_C (\infty) \ = \ & U_A \quad & \longrightarrow \ \text{check.} & \end{align} $$
    So we start at $ U_C (0) $ and end at $ U_A $, at which point $ \ q_C \ = \ {C U_A} \ $ — it's constant so $ \ I (t) \ = \ {dq_C / dt} \ = \ 0 \ $.
    If $ \quad U_C (0) \ = \ 0 \quad $ then: $ \quad U_C (t) \ = \ {U_A \bigl( 1 - \exp (-t / RC) \bigr)} $
    In plain Frenglish: a DC generator connected to an $ RC $ circuit will draw current until the voltage across the capacitor equals the voltage across the generator. This never strictly happens: there's an asymptote in $ U_A $ ad infinitum and the curve is exponential.
    Or maybe it's logarithmic... Hard — pointless? — to say with all the negative signs.
    Better look at it (and play with the parameters):
    What does this mean in terms of analogue signal?
    First of all, we've silenced $ \ U_R (t) \ $ — but $ \ U_A (t) \ = \ {U_C (t) + U_R (t)} \ $ means $ \ U_R (t) \ = \ \bigl( U_A - U_C (0) \bigr) \exp (-t / RC) \ $.
    At $ \ {t = \infty} \ $ this gives zero. Which makes sense: $ \ U_R (t) \ = \ RC dU_C / dt \ $ (remember?), so once $ \ U_C (t) \ $ equals $ U_A $ and becomes constant, its rate of change becomes $ \ dU_C / dt \ = \ 0 \ $, so $ \ U_R (t) \ = \ RC $ times zero... so zero.
    Another way to get this result was: $ \ U_C (\infty) \ = \ U_A \ $ and $ \ U_R (t) \ = \ {U_A - U_C (t)} \ $ so $ \ U_R (\infty) \ = \ {U_A - U_A} \ = \ 0 \ $.
    Ok. Now an analogue signal being a voltage measure, if our output signal is $ U_C $ (the voltage across the capacitor), it will converge very fast to our constant input signal ($ U_A $). The greater the value of $ RC $, the slower the convergence. Indeed: $ \ -t / RC \ $ is inversely proportional to $ RC $.
    A constant voltage is a DC offset. $ \ U_C (t) \ $ will not block DC: it will progressively rebuild it: a fade-in with an exponential curve.
    Next: what if we take the output across the resistor?
    $ U_R (t) \ = \ {\bigl( U_A - U_C (0) \bigr) \exp (-t / RC)} $
    $ {-t / RC} \ = \ {-\omega_c t} \ $ and $ \ U_A - U_C (0) \ $ is constant, so the chain rule and the constant factor rule give:
    $$ dU_R / dt \ = \ -\omega_c \bigl( U_A - U_C (0) \bigr) \exp (-\omega_c t) \ = \ \bigl( U_C (0) - U_A \bigr) \omega_c \exp (-\omega_c t) $$
    The rate of change is exponentially decaying. Not a crucial information but I guess I like Calculus now.
    What actually matters: $ \ U_R (0) \ = \ {\bigl( U_A - U_C (0) \bigr) \exp (-0 / RC)} \ = \ {U_A - U_C (0)} \quad $ (or $ U_A $ if $ \ U_C (0) \ = \ 0 \ $).
    We already know that $ \ U_R (\infty) \ = \ 0 \ $.
    So we start with an initial voltage $ \ U_A - U_C (0) \ $ and converge very fast to zero. Again: how fast grows with $ \omega_c $ (same reason as before).
